What Does Ocean Acidification Mean?

Ocean Acidification, the silent threat to Marine life and Human health, refers to the issue of decreasing pH levels in marine areas, which causes significant threats to the ecosystem and our well-being. In this article, we will go over the causes of Acidification, the impact it has on marine life and human health, as well as the strategies we can adopt to mitigate this rising issue.

Causes of Ocean Acidification

It will come as a surprise to no one that Ocean Acidification is directly linked to the industrialization of the planet caused by us. With the increase in human activities such as burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and the destruction of coral reefs. Since the Industrial Revolution in the 1700s, the ocean’s surface pH level has decreased by approximately 0.1 units, but it is projected to decrease by 0.3-0.4 more units by the end of this century. If this change seems small, please refer to this explanation of the pH scale.

In short, the pH scale is a logarithmic scale from 0 to 14 that represents the acidity or basicity of aqueous solutions, with 7 representing neutral solutions that are neither acidic nor basic. The average ocean pH was 8.2 prior to the Industrial Revolution, with a value of around 8.1 at present.

The Impact on Marine Life

Calcifying Organisms

Many ocean species such as shellfish, corals, and some plankton rely on Calcium Carbonate to build their shells and skeletons. However, with the increase in acidity, the availability of free carbonate ions is reduced drastically, harming these organisms’ ability to form and maintain their shells. This leads to weakened shells, reduced growth rates, and overall increased mortality.

Larval Development

The early stages of many marine organisms including fish and various invertebrates are particularly sensitive to changes in acidity, causing them to struggle to develop properly. This may seem inconsequential, but this can cause disruptions to the entire food chain.

Ecosystem Disruption

Ocean acidification can alter the composition of marine ecosystems, leading to changes in species distribution and abundance. This may have a domino effect on marine biodiversity and ecosystem resilience.

The Impact on Human Health

Seafood Availability and Quality

Ocean Acidification can cause degradation in the quality and quantity of seafood, affecting the livelihoods of communities that are dependent on fishing, as well as the communities that rely on seafood as a primary source of protein. As an example, changes in the ocean’s chemistry can harm the levels on essential nutrients like omega-3 fatty acids in fish.

Toxicity and Contaminants

Acidified waters may increase the availability of harmful substances such as heavy metals, leading to higher exposure for humans who consume contaminated seafood.

The Steps to avoid an Acid-Filled Future

Reducing CO2 Emissions

The most effective way to tackle ocean acidity is to reduce atmospheric CO2 levels by switching to renewable energy sources and implementing sustainable land-use practices.

Support Marine Conservation

Establishing and maintaining protected marine areas can help preserve biodiversity and support ecosystem resilience.

Research and Monitoring

Continued Scientific research is essential to understand the effects of ocean acidification and developing effective strategies to adapt to a changing world.
